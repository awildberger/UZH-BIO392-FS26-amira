Answer for the questions (paper): 
1. Looking at CNVs of healthy people to see what genetic changes (CNVs) are normal (benign) and which are linked to / cause diseases (high risk or pathogenic). This is why we need a baseline: to see if a DNA change in a patient is just a normal human variation or if the change (in DNA) may be linked to a disease (adverse phenotypes) (distincion of pathogenic variants from benign ones). The map is what serves as this baseline/reference. Including various ethnicities is critical because genomic background, including ethnicity, can influence thresholds. Without ethnically diverse controls, a benign variant in one population might be mistakenly classified as pathogenic for someone in another population. The paper specifically pinpoints an example of alpha-amylase, which is an example showing that a trait can be either adaptive or maladaptive given the environmental context. 

2. The authors define the size of copy number variations (CNVs) as genomic segments of at least 50 base pairs (bp) that differ in copy number when comparing two or more genomes. The map catalogues variants in the size range 50kb - 3 mb. Elements smaller than 50kb are classified as insertions/deletions (indels). 

3. Microarrays and Next generation sequencing (NGS) are the primary approaches used for CNV detection.

Microarrays (including CGH and SNP based arrays): 
Advantages are: well suited for studying quantitative variants; array CGH is more sensitive for detecting duplications and small differences in copy number. 
Disadvantages are: Many platforms lack probes in segmental duplication regions, causing them to miss disease-relevant CNVs (for example beta-defensin cluster in psoriasis or Crohns). Lower breakpoint resolution (less precise, can only localize breakpoints to large genomic regions) compared to sequencing. Earlier platforms like BAC CGH tended to overestimate breakpoints and miss small variants. SNP-based arrays miss more duplications than array CGH. 
Comparative Genomic Hybridization (CGH): An array-based technique that compares a test genome to a reference to find deletions or duplications. 
SNP (single-nucleotide polymorphism) array is a lab tool that scans DNA to detect: single-letter changes in DNA (like A → G at one position), not origincally designed for CNVs. 

Next generation sequencing: are sequencing based methods.
Advantages are: High sensitivity, high resolution: accurate sequence-level breakpoint resolution ("Sanger sequencing can detect CNV breakpoints with single-nucleotide resolution"). The 1000 Genomes Project was able to perform whole-genome sequencing of multiple samples from many populations. 
Disadvantages are: Biased toward detecting deletions, may miss duplications (more than array CGH). Private or rare variants may be missed.

The 2 criteria for clustering are number of distinct subjects (number of individuals carrying the variant) and number of distinct studies (number of independent studies containing at least one variant in the cluster). This helps reduce false positives (variants supported by more subjects are less likely to be false positives), excludes singletons, excludes study-specific artefacts, but can also lead to the removal of rare pathogenic variants. This filtering apporach defines the inclusive vs a stringent maps. 
Number of subjects filter removes singletons (relevant for next question, explanation)
Number of studies filter removes study-specific artefacts. 

5.  Stringency level 1: At least 1 subject AND 1 study. It includes all reported variants (CNV and CNVR), also singletons. This is the most permissive level and represents the raw combined data.

Inclusive map (stringency level 2): At least 2 subjects AND 1 study. It removes singletons but still allows variants seen in just one study, capturing a broader range of variation including rarer but potentially real CNVs.

Stringent map (stringency level 12): At least 2 subjects AND 2 studies. It requires independent replication across studies,  left with only the most reliably supported varaints with reduced platform bias.
 So, Inclusive = broader, noisier, more complete but less certain vs Stringent = smaller, cleaner, more trustworthy dataset. 

6. in inclusive: 9.5% of the genome varies (7.5% losses, 3.9% gains) (contributes to CNVs)
in stringent: 4.8% of the genome vaires (3.6% losses, 2.3% gains)( contributes to CNVs)

7. Intuition may suggest that protein-coding genes are more variable because of their large diversity. However, the paper suggests that the non-coding genes are more variable. there is negative selection for "important" (protein coding) genes, meaning that here, there are few CNVs happening (as these would be disease causing), so if changing the number of copies of a gene causes harm, evolution tends to “remove” those changes (negative/purifying selection). Vs in (exons of) non-coding regions: are under less constraint, instead are more variable in copy number as changes in these regions are more tolerable (=CNVs in these regions are less harmful). (also note there are "hotspots" for variability; at end and center of the chromosomes). Also showed that deletions are more harmful (result in more diseases) than duplications, possibly because this can remove the gene's function entirely (like "erasing instructions"). 

8. There are also homozygous deletions (107, 99 in the stringent map): genes you can completely lose (and still have a healthy phenotype). This shows that these genes are not essential for basic survival. Their functions include: age related phenotypes (UDP glucuronosyltransferase), blood type determination (RHD), delayed AIDS from HIV-1 infection (KIR3DL1), susceptibility to multiple sclerosis (HLA-DRB1), autoimmune diseases (ie HLA-DQA1 for celiac), or placental function and fetal wellbeing (pregnancy-specific beta-1-glycoprotein 1). 
The authors labeled these genes "non-essential" because they can be entirely absent from the genome without causing immediate or obvious adverse health effects in the individuals studied. Many of these genes have paralogues, suggesting their function may be redundant and compensated for by other similar genes. Where known, phenotypic context may be late-onset or context dependent. 

9. For doctors this may be an important tool to help them with diagnosing patients, so to see if a DNA change is normal (benign/not worrying) or if it may be disease-causing. Thereby helping with risk assessment and determining whether a patient is at risk or has a certain disease based on their CNVs (and comparison to controls like parents, siblings or population). I would use the CNV map (taking into account the genomic background) to see if the patients CNVs overlap with the map. If so, it is likely to be benign. I would also check the 935 medically relevant genes found by the authors of this paper that involve CNVs. A CNV absent from the map and overlapping a disease-associated gene would strongly suggest clinical significance. Important to remember is that there are no official threshold, as ethnicities (in general genomic backgrounds) make each individual unique (helpful tools,ballpark figures, but not definitive categories/diagnoses).
